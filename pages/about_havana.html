History of Havana

Explore the rich and vibrant history of Havana, from its founding in 1519 to its status as a UNESCO World Heritage Site. Dive into the fascinating journey of this historic city.

Founding of Havana

Havana's storied past can be traced back to November 16, 1519, when the Spanish explorer Diego Velázquez, acting on behalf of the Spanish monarchy, established the city's third and final settlement, which still stands today.

Throughout the 16th century, Havana endured repeated destruction at the hands of French pirates and corsairs. To safeguard its future, the Spanish Crown invested in military defenses at the entrance of Havana Bay, fortifying the city as the best-protected settlement in the New World.

Havana in the XVII Century

The 17th century witnessed significant expansion in Havana, marked by the construction of imposing civil and religious edifices. Notable additions included the San Agustin convent, the completion of El Morro Castle, the Dorotea de la Luna fountain in La Chorrera, the Santo Angel Custodio church, the San Lazaro hospital, the Santa Teresa monastery, the San Felipe Neri convent, and the 1728 establishment of the Royal and Pontifical University of San Jeronimo within the confines of the San Juan de Letran convent.

Havana in the XVIII Century

By the mid-18th century, Havana boasted a population exceeding 70,000 residents. On June 6, 1762, Havana faced a formidable British fleet comprising over 50 ships and 14,000 soldiers. After a two-month siege, the English forces captured the city, under the command of Sir Georges Keppel, who ruled for eleven months.

In mid-1763, Havana was returned to Spanish control in exchange for Florida. As Spain's trade monopoly waned, Havana's prosperity soared, eventually becoming a free port in 1818. The city embraced luxury, fashion, and cultural vibrancy, earning it the moniker "Paris of the Antilles."

Havana in the XIX Century

The early 19th century witnessed the arrival of the German naturalist Alexander Von Humboldt, who marveled at the vitality of Havana's port. In 1837, Havana inaugurated the first railway section, spanning 51 kilometers between Havana and Güines, primarily for sugar transportation. This marked Cuba's transition to becoming the fifth country globally with a railway system. Throughout the century, Havana flourished with cultural hubs like the opulent Teatro Tacon and the Coliseo Theater.

The late 19th century saw the conclusion of Spanish colonization in Cuba, with Havana entering the 20th century under U.S. occupation following the sinking of the American battleship Maine in its port, which led to the United States' involvement in the island's affairs.

Havana in the XX Century

In the 1930s, under American influence, Havana experienced substantial growth with the construction of sumptuous hotels, casinos, and magnificent nightclubs like the Focsa, Habana Libre, and Hotel Nacional. Havana became a hub of gaming and indulgence.

However, with Fidel Castro Ruz's victory in the 1959 Revolution, profound social transformations unfolded, particularly in education, public health, and infrastructure development. A comprehensive network of educational institutions and healthcare services was established, ensuring accessibility for all citizens.

Havana Today

Havana's historic center earned the distinction of being designated a National Monument by the Cuban Government in 1976 and a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1982.

Havana's global appeal was further validated when it was named one of the New7Wonders Cities in 2014. The city's enchanting ambiance, warm hospitality, and the jovial spirit of its residents were celebrated when it officially received its status as one of the modern world's seven wonders in 2016. A monument and commemorative plaque were unveiled at the San Salvador de la Punta Fortress, an integral part of Havana's UNESCO heritage, in honor of this prestigious recognition.
